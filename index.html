THE ELEMENTS

Pong is one of the first games to achieve commercial succes. As a product of it’s time, being build in 1972, it’s UI is simple. It is build up out of a minimal ammount of pixels, protraying a simple ping pong table. Below we will get into the elements more closely

[]The playingfield

The playing field consists of a ball, a line in the middle, the paddles and a scoreboard. The ball is something the players use to get points. The line splits the playing field in two. This to visually communicate which side is which. The paddles are the main elements the user’s control. The scoreboard communicates how much points each player has.

[]The paddle

The paddles are the UI element the user directly controls. It can move up and down on one axis, being often the y axis. The paddle’s purpose is to hit the ‘ball’ that is being hit around the playing field. It can do this only with the side facing towards the other paddle.

[]The ball

The ball is an element that the user indirectly controls. It can move on two axes’, being the x and y axes. Player can only ‘hit the ball’ with the paddle, after which the ball follows a trajectory based on bouncing around the field. The other player, if able, bounces the ball the other way. When the ball enters the space behind on of the users’ paddle, the opposite user gets one point.

[]The scoreboard

The scoreboard is also indirectly controlled by the players. Every time the ball gets behind one player’s paddle’, the other gains one point. The scoreboard is there to invoke competition between the two players. The player with the most points will win. The game does not have as specified end time. It is up to the players deciding when the game has ended.

[]The controls

The physical controls usually exist of two inputs for the paddles. In later versions that were adapted for home usage, there were also controls to start and, stop and reset the game. The controls for the paddles usually are knobs that can be turned. Later versions also used joysticks.

[]The sound

The game also produces sound. It plays a ‘plonk’ sound when the balls gets hit by a paddle. This to indicate to the player that the ball has been successfully hit. It also plays an ‘skrrsk’ sound when the ball hits on of the players void behind their paddle. This to indicate that the players has failed to deflect.

Variating on pong

After Pong, Atari kept on developing more digital arcade games based on Pong. Quickly they would figure out how to do more complicated graphics, leaving pong in their long legacy. A couple of pong likes are here to keep in mind while making Plong.

[] Quadrapong

Released in 1974, this remix of pong gives room for up to four players to play pong simultaneously. The original arcade machine had the controls placed on the four sides of it. This version would make an hectic version of Pong.

[]Rebound

Rebound is a pong-like released in 1974. In this game two players have to bounce the ball over the net. If the ball hits the ground of one player, the other one gets a point. Depending on the lenght of the turn, the net will become heigher. As soon as the ball hits the ground, the net resets.

[]Breakout

This game was released in 1976. Unlike the original Pong, this game is played by one player. The player has to use the paddle to bounce the ball against rectangles above. When the ball hits one of the rectangles, it bounces and the rectangle gets destroyed. They will also be rewared a point. The players loses if the ball hits the ground.

What is Plong?

Plong is pong being played across the dommel in Eindhoven. So it is basically pong on a big scale. What makes Plong, Plong, is that it will be powered by plant batteries. These plant batteries are based on the tech by plant.e. We will use the batteries to drive the screen and other components. This way Plong will be powered by plants. Transferring Pong to Plong.

Why is Plong being made?

Plong is being developed for Glow, a festival celebrating art made with light. The festival is hosted all over Eindhoven, a city in Noord Brabant in the Netherlands. The sculptures on the festival are made by artist, companies and students. Plong will be developed by students from Fontys ICT deeltijd. They have taken on the challege to make a project for Glow based on Plant.e tech.

What controller type fits GLOW?

For this project one UI student took on themselves the design of the controller. But before they could design, they had to look at for who they were designing. As such they researched into who came GLOW, what inputs they like for controlling a digital product, what inputs there are and how to relate to the end user. They also looked how the inputs would relate to the environmental factors of GLOW. Out of that research came the advise to use different controller inputs. Below is the main conclusion of said research:

“The audience for GLOW is broad and general, appealing to a wide demographic that includes both regional and national visitors, and even some international interest. Insights from previous attendees and projects confirm this broad appeal, indicating that GLOW attracts people of all ages. Given Eindhoven's demographic, particularly the 25-45 age group, and their digital proficiency, it is important to design Plong with simple digital interfaces that can be easily used by this diverse audience.

Survey results indicate that people prefer interfaces they use daily, such as keyboards, labelled buttons, and touchscreens, which are familiar and widely adopted. In contrast, motion and voice controls are less popular and less understood. To ensure an intuitive user experience for our controller, we should choose simple, well-known inputs to avoid the need for tutorials and prevent user frustration due to complexity or unreliability.

Plong requires three types of inputs for gameplay: one to move the paddle, one to signal player readiness, and an admin button to turn the game on and off. These inputs ensure players can control the game and indicate their readiness, while the administrator can start, restart, or shut down the game. With these basic inputs, all necessary controls for Plong are effectively covered.

From the table of goodness, (see table pictured below) based on the information gathered about the inputs, I have five possible inputs that rank the highest for the paddle control. These are Buttons, a DDR style pad, a Turnable knob, a steeringwheel and a joystick/slider. These inputs can be considered the best theoretical fit for PLONG on GLOW. And should give a good user experience for the people attending GLOW.”

the Joystick

The concept drawings

The joystick was made with one axis in mind. When the stick would be moved to the left, the paddle would move to the left. When it moved to the right, the paddle would move to the left. The paddle was not analog, meaning that it did not register pressure.

The controller would work by the joystick element pushing on the left or right button. Internally it would have a seesaw that would up and down corresponding to the movement of the stick. To give it a way to spring back to the middle, a small flat needle was made to act as a spring. The basic set up is seen in the drawing on the left. Note that the needle was not initially in this drawing.

Which controller did we choose for Plong?

Eventually we had to choose one controller to develop further into the main prototype for Plong. The controller designer showed the group two different designs. One being the cattail and the other the tree trunk. After some discussion and asking the student expert Niels van der burg (he had a project on GLOW once), we chose the broadleaf cattail.

Why did we choose the broadleaf Cattail?

The cattail was preferred for four reasons. The controller was more eyecatching. This because it was a big pole that was visible from a lot of angles. Secondly it was fairly simple in concept. We could scale it fairly easily because of it simplicity. Thirdly it fitted the theme good. This because the cat tail is a common riverside plant. Lastly the controller had a low amount of breakable parts. This version only had a pot meter as weak point.

The rotatable stump was excluded because it fit the theme less well. Additionally, it was difficult to integrate into the setup we envisioned for GLOW. This was because it is a controller that needs to be placed on an elevated surface, which was less of an issue for the cat tail.

How was the controller developed further?

The broadleaf cattail was scaled into a bigger prototype. Now it was aproxamiteally 1 and a half meters long. The mechanism was also changed, which I will discuss below. The styling was lost however this time. This because we developed with prototype first, styling later mindset.

The eventual prototype

The cat's tail consists of several components. The technical components were discussed in the previous paragraph. However, it will be mentioned here which of those components are included in the controller. The cat's tail is made up of three groups:

  • The grip
  • The arm with a rotating axis at the bottom, connected to the ground with elastic
  • A slider potentiometer that is connected to the arm with a hook. The whole group is schematically represented as below:

Below, each component group is described. Specifications such as length are also discussed for each component. The grip/handle The grip is shaped like a cat's tail. On the left side, there are four slots where the fingers fit. At a 45-degree angle to the right, there is a lowered section with a bar where the heart rate monitor fits. This can lead the cables from the heart rate monitor down through a hole that runs into the hollow tube of the arm. The grip has a diameter of 4 cm. It is 16 cm high. In the middle, at the bottom of the grip's length, there is a hole with a diameter of 1.7 cm. The grip is made with a PVC tube of 1.6 cm in mind. The grip has a 0.1 cm clearance, making it relatively loose. For further development, I recommend making the diameter smaller if possible. However, I was limited by the accuracy of the 3D printer and couldn't immediately test if it would fit over the arm.

The arm and rotating axis The arm serves as the movable element that transmits the user's movement to the rest of the controller. The arm we used is a PVC tube of 1.7 cm. The length is approximately one and a half meters. The arm is attached to the controller setup with a rotating axis at the bottom of the arm's length, allowing the arm to rotate left or right. At the bottom of the arm, in the base plate where the controller is built, there is a hook and elastic. The elastic acts as a force that pulls the arm back to the neutral point. If the controller is pushed to the left, the elastic will automatically bring it back to the center. Additionally, the elastic ensures that the controller remains upright when not in use.

The slider potentiometer The slider potentiometer is the component that registers the movement of the rotating arm and sends it back to the computer controlling Plong. The pot slider is connected to the rotating arm with a hook. When the rotating arm is pulled to the left, the hook pushes the connected pot slider to the left. If it goes to the right, it is pulled to the right again. At rest, the potentiometer is always in the center because the rotating arm is pulled to the center by the elastic.

Final advise for further development

How to make the controller vandal-proof and GLOW-proof?

How to make the controller vandal-proof and GLOW-proof? The current controller is quite fragile in construction. The connection to the potentiometer can easily overstress the potentiometer. Additionally, the arm made of a PVC pipe is also quite fragile. The electronics are now exposed. Especially with the rain that falls in November, this is not advisable. I recommend that future developers improve the controller in this area.

How can a heart rate monitor be better integrated into the controller grip?

At the moment, the board on which the heart rate monitor is mounted is exposed to the user. Given the circumstances of GLOW, this is, of course, not advisable. I recommend that those further developing the controller integrate the heart rate monitor better into the controller grip, where it is behind a protective layer. This way, the board is no longer exposed.

How can the controller be better styled to fit the Plong theme?

The controller is currently quite bare in terms of styling. It does not yet look like a cat's tail that you would find next to a shore. I recommend the next designer work together with the vandal-proof designer. Together, you can ensure that the design is vandal-proof and GLOW-proof, but also fits within the theme.
